# Team Alignment

## Overview
Architecture only works if the teams building the system understand and follow it. Alignment means creating shared mental models — not enforcing compliance top-down.

## Core concepts

### Share the "why"
Teams that understand the reasoning behind decisions are more likely to apply them correctly in novel situations. Document not just what was decided, but why.

### Architecture as a conversation
- Involve team members early in architectural discussions.
- Use collaborative formats like architecture reviews, design sessions, or RFC processes.
- Avoid presenting decisions as done facts when input is still valuable.

### Lightweight governance
- Define and communicate guardrails (what must not change) rather than dictating every detail.
- Let teams make local decisions within those boundaries.
- Use ADRs to make decisions visible without requiring sign-off on everything.

### Consistent vocabulary
A shared glossary reduces misunderstandings across teams. Define key terms explicitly and use them consistently in diagrams, documentation, and conversations.

### Feedback loops
- Regular architecture syncs help catch drift early.
- Make it easy for teams to raise concerns or propose changes.
- Treat misalignment as a signal that communication or documentation needs improvement.

## Decision considerations / trade-offs
- More alignment processes add overhead; too little leads to divergence.
- Central control keeps systems coherent but reduces team ownership.
- Written documentation scales better than verbal alignment but can go stale.
