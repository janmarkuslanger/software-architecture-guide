# Stakeholder Management

## Overview
Architects work across organizational boundaries. Effective stakeholder management ensures that architecture decisions reflect real constraints, earn trust, and get implemented.

## Core concepts

### Identify stakeholders
Different roles have different concerns:
- **Business / Product**: time-to-market, cost, features, compliance.
- **Development teams**: productivity, clarity, autonomy, technical debt.
- **Operations / SRE**: reliability, deployability, observability.
- **Security**: risk exposure, data protection, compliance.
- **Executives**: strategic alignment, risk, budget.

### Understand concerns
Each stakeholder has quality attribute priorities that may conflict. The architect's job is to surface these early, not resolve them unilaterally.

### Communicate in their language
- Engineers: diagrams, ADRs, code examples.
- Product / Business: impact on features, time, cost, and risk.
- Executives: executive summaries, risk exposure, strategic fit.

### Manage expectations
- State assumptions explicitly.
- Make trade-offs visible rather than hiding them.
- Set realistic expectations about what architecture can and cannot solve.

### Build ongoing relationships
Stakeholder management is continuous, not a one-time activity. Regular check-ins prevent surprises and build alignment over time.

## Decision considerations / trade-offs
- Over-involving stakeholders slows decisions; under-involving them causes rework.
- Transparency about trade-offs builds trust but may increase scrutiny.
- Prioritizing one stakeholder group risks alienating others — balance is required.
